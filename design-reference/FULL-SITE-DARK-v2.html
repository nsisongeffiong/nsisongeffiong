"What the delta teaches about forgetting"

Nsisong EffiongApril 10, 2026
The river does not mournthe banks it has abandoned—it is already elsewhere,already becomingsomething the sea will name.
I have stood at three deltas.Each one taught methe same thingin a different mouth.
Forgetting is not failure.It is the water findinga lower place to go,a softer way through.
What I have lostis not behind me—it is distributed,given to the silt,the estuarine grass,the slow birdswho do not keepwhat they find.
The delta does not askwhere it is going.It only spreads,and spreads,and thinsinto the open.
· · ·

Poet's note

Written after returning to the Niger Delta for the first time in seven years. The geography kept insisting on becoming metaphor — I eventually stopped resisting it.

The stage routing model

# Stage dispatch def run_stage(stage_num, task, prior=None):   response = call_api(get_model(stage_num), task, prior)   write_files(response)   git_commit(f"stage-{stage_num}")   return response

Each stage receives the full output of the previous stage as context. This lets Stage 4 make meaningful corrections.

Why public sector AI adoption keeps failing — and who benefits from the story we tell about it

Governments are adopting AI tools at pace, yet most deployments quietly collapse within 18 months. The dominant explanation — institutional slowness — obscures something more structural.

Nsisong EffiongApril 2, 202614 min read

In 2023, a mid-sized European municipality deployed an AI triage system for its social services department. By early 2025 it had been quietly retired — not because of technical failure, but because the vendor had defined success metrics that diverged steadily from what social workers actually needed.

The failure modes cluster around the interface between what vendors are incentivised to measure and what public value actually requires.

This story is not unusual. Across healthcare, education, and benefits administration, AI deployments that arrived with fanfare have been wound down while stakeholders wait for contracts to expire.

I.

The procurement structure creates the failure mode

The public body lacks technical capacity to evaluate what it is buying — and the procurement process is not designed to build that capacity.

This creates a structural asymmetry that precedes any question of institutional culture or risk appetite.

II.

"The question is not whether AI will transform governance — it is who gets to define what transformation means, and for whom."

— Nsisong Effiong

The framing of public institutions as congenitally unable to adopt technology performs specific work. It positions the solution as more private sector involvement, faster procurement, reduced oversight — precisely the conditions that created the failure mode in the first place.
